the last diplomat on the beltway

there is a car that circles the beltway every thursday. i know this because i've seen it. diplomatic plates, country code i won't mention, same exit every time. it's not the plate that interests me — it's the routine. the ritual of it. the way bureaucracy becomes habit becomes something almost human.

i started logging plates three years ago out of boredom. what it became is harder to describe. a record of presence. proof that specific people from specific countries were, at some point, here. this particular stretch of pavement. this city that exists mostly as a backdrop to other people's importance.

diplomacy is the art of letting someone else have your way.

the plates don't tell you much. a number, a country, a color scheme. what they don't tell you is what was discussed at the meeting that morning, or whether the car is heading home or running from something, or whether the person in the back seat is thinking about dinner or about war.

but i keep logging them anyway. because the alternative is not knowing, and i've never been comfortable with that.
